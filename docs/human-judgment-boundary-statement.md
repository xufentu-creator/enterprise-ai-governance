## Abstract

This document defines the human judgment boundary within Xufen Tu’s research on human judgment as a structural constraint in AI-mediated complex systems.

The purpose of this statement is to clarify where artificial intelligence may support analysis, classification, prediction, recommendation, and coordination, and where human judgment, responsibility, and accountable decision-making must remain present.

In this research, human judgment is not treated as a vague personal opinion.

It is treated as a structural boundary that helps prevent complex systems from moving faster than their ability to evaluate uncertainty, consequence, responsibility, and correction.

---

## Core Position

AI systems may assist decisions.

AI systems may classify information, summarize documents, generate options, identify patterns, recommend actions, rank choices, and accelerate workflows.

However, AI systems do not automatically carry responsibility for consequences.

Where uncertainty, risk, consequence, social impact, ethical judgment, legal accountability, or irreversible action is involved, human judgment remains necessary as a structural boundary.

```text
AI may support.
AI may recommend.
AI may accelerate.
AI may organize.

But AI should not fully replace judgment where responsibility remains human, organizational, legal, or social.
```

This boundary is central to the research position:

**Human judgment remains a structural constraint in AI-mediated complex systems.**

---

## Human Judgment as Structural Constraint

Human judgment functions as a structural constraint when it slows, redirects, reviews, or limits system momentum before action is taken.

A constraint is not a rejection of progress.

A constraint is a condition that helps prevent collapse, drift, or uncontrolled acceleration.

In complex systems, speed can create risk when decisions move faster than accountability, verification, correction, or responsibility.

Human judgment becomes necessary when automated outputs cannot fully evaluate:

```text
Context
Uncertainty
Consequence
Responsibility
Timing
Exception
Ethical weight
Local conditions
Social impact
Long-term effects
```

The more complex the environment, the more important the judgment boundary becomes.

---

## Final Decision Boundary

The final decision boundary is the point where responsibility for action must remain with a human, organization, institution, or other accountable authority.

AI may generate options, but a final decision requires responsibility.

AI may recommend, but a responsible actor must determine whether the recommendation should be accepted, modified, delayed, escalated, or rejected.

AI may accelerate information flow, but acceleration does not remove the need for judgment.

```text
Recommendation is not responsibility.
Prediction is not accountability.
Automation is not judgment.
Output is not decision.
Speed is not correctness.
```

A system becomes structurally risky when automated outputs are treated as final decisions without a clear accountable boundary.

---

## Non-Transferable Responsibility

Non-transferable responsibility refers to responsibility that cannot be fully moved from humans or organizations to automated systems.

AI can support decision-making, but it does not fully absorb responsibility for outcomes.

This is especially important when decisions affect:

```text
Rights
Safety
Finance
Health
Employment
Education
Legal status
Public services
Business operations
Customer trust
Personal identity
Community impact
```

In these situations, responsibility must remain connected to accountable actors.

AI may be part of the decision environment, but it should not become a place where responsibility disappears.

---

## Human Confirmation Layer

A human confirmation layer is a process step in which AI-assisted, automated, or structured outputs are reviewed before action becomes final.

This layer helps prevent automated outputs from becoming unreviewed decisions.

In applied systems, a human confirmation layer may appear as:

```text
Manual review
Provider confirmation
Customer confirmation
Manager approval
Professional assessment
Legal review
Human escalation
Final authorization
Correction check
Service boundary confirmation
```

The human confirmation layer does not mean every task must be slow or manual.

It means that when consequences matter, a responsible actor must remain visible before action is finalized.

---

## Judgment Capacity

Judgment capacity refers to the ability of a person, organization, or system to evaluate complexity before acting.

Judgment capacity includes:

```text
Attention
Experience
Context awareness
Responsibility awareness
Ability to pause
Ability to question
Ability to compare
Ability to correct
Ability to refuse acceleration
Ability to carry consequence
```

In high-speed systems, judgment capacity can become overwhelmed.

When automated outputs, recommendations, or actions scale faster than human judgment can evaluate them, structural risk increases.

This relationship can be summarized as:

```text
Structural Risk ∝ Momentum / Judgment Capacity
```

The stronger the system momentum and the weaker the judgment capacity, the higher the structural risk.

---

## Momentum and Risk

Momentum refers to the speed and scale at which information, recommendations, decisions, or actions move through a system.

Momentum is not always negative.

Momentum can create efficiency, coordination, growth, and access.

However, momentum becomes risky when it exceeds the system’s ability to verify, judge, correct, and take responsibility.

```text
More speed does not always mean more intelligence.
More automation does not always mean more accountability.
More output does not always mean better decision-making.
More scale does not always mean more stability.
```

This is why the principle of **Judgment Before Momentum** matters.

Before a system accelerates, it should have enough judgment capacity to carry the consequences of that acceleration.

## Decision Architecture and Judgment

Decision architecture refers to the structure through which information becomes action.

Human judgment does not exist outside decision architecture.

It is shaped by:

```text
Available information
System design
Authority structure
Responsibility boundaries
Incentives
Review steps
Escalation paths
Platform recommendations
Organizational pressure
Time constraints
```

A weak decision architecture can make human judgment appear present while actually reducing it.

For example, if a human is asked to approve an automated recommendation without enough context, time, authority, or ability to challenge the output, the human may become a symbolic reviewer rather than a real decision-maker.

A meaningful judgment boundary requires real capacity to evaluate, revise, delay, or reject.

---

## Responsibility Drift

Responsibility drift occurs when responsibility becomes weakened, displaced, or fragmented across people, organizations, platforms, models, and automated processes.

AI-mediated systems can increase responsibility drift when decision influence is distributed but accountability is unclear.

Responsibility drift may appear when:

```text
A platform says the model recommended it.
A team says the system generated it.
A manager says the data supported it.
A vendor says the client approved it.
A user says the platform guided them.
An organization says the process was automated.
```

In each case, responsibility may move away from any clearly accountable actor.

Human judgment boundaries help reduce responsibility drift by making review, authority, and accountability visible.

---

## AI Support Boundary

AI support is valuable when it improves understanding, organization, comparison, and analysis.

AI support becomes risky when it is treated as final authority without accountability.

The support boundary can be understood as follows:

```text
AI can assist with classification.
Humans remain responsible for final interpretation.

AI can assist with recommendation.
Humans remain responsible for accepting or rejecting action.

AI can assist with prediction.
Humans remain responsible for evaluating uncertainty and consequence.

AI can assist with documentation.
Humans remain responsible for accuracy, context, and attribution.

AI can assist with workflow.
Humans and organizations remain responsible for outcomes.
```

This distinction allows AI to be useful without allowing responsibility to disappear.

## Boundary Conditions

A human judgment boundary becomes especially important under certain conditions.

```text
High uncertainty
High consequence
High speed
High scale
Weak accountability
Conflicting outputs
Irreversible action
Sensitive identity
Legal or regulatory exposure
Health or safety impact
Financial risk
Public trust impact
Cross-system dependency
```

When these conditions appear, AI-supported decisions require stronger human confirmation, clearer responsibility structure, and more explicit decision architecture.

## Irreducible Judgment

Irreducible judgment refers to forms of human evaluation that cannot be fully reduced to data processing, prediction, ranking, or automated recommendation.

This does not mean that AI cannot contribute.

It means that certain decisions involve contextual responsibility that cannot be carried by automated outputs alone.

Irreducible judgment appears when decisions require evaluation of:

```text
Meaning
Context
Timing
Exception
Responsibility
Consequence
Human impact
Institutional duty
Ethical trade-offs
Long-term stability
```

In these cases, AI may support the process, but the judgment boundary remains human or institutionally accountable.

---

## Structural Stability

Structural stability refers to a system’s ability to remain understandable, accountable, and correctable under speed, scale, complexity, or automation.

A system may continue operating while becoming structurally unstable.

This can happen when:

```text
Decisions become faster but less reviewable.
Outputs become larger but less accountable.
Processes become automated but less understandable.
Responsibilities become distributed but less visible.
Systems become efficient but harder to correct.
```

Human judgment contributes to structural stability by preserving the ability to pause, evaluate, correct, and take responsibility.

## Relationship to Provenance

Human judgment is also connected to provenance.

When ideas, decisions, documents, recommendations, or outputs are detached from origin, responsibility becomes weaker.

Provenance helps preserve:

```text
Who created the idea
Where the record exists
When the concept appeared
How the version developed
How the decision was formed
Where responsibility remains located
```

In AI-mediated environments, provenance supports judgment by keeping origin, authorship, context, and responsibility traceable.

Without provenance, automated systems can repeat or recombine outputs while weakening the relationship between meaning and source.

## Relationship to Applied Systems

In applied systems, the human judgment boundary may appear in practical forms.

For example, in a structured service request system:

```text
A customer submits a request.
The system organizes the information.
AI or automation may help classify the request.
A human confirmation layer reviews the request.
A provider confirms scope, price, timing, and responsibility.
The customer confirms the next step.
The final service decision remains accountable.
```

This shows how AI support can be integrated without removing human judgment.

Applied systems should make the judgment boundary visible rather than hiding it behind automation.

---

## Applied Boundary Example

A service platform may help organize customer requests, route information, and support communication.

However, the platform should not automatically replace professional judgment, provider confirmation, customer consent, or final responsibility.

A responsible service flow should clarify:

```text
What the customer requested
What information was submitted
Who receives the request
Who reviews the request
Who confirms the service scope
Who provides the final quote
Who performs the service
Who is responsible for the outcome
```

This is an example of human judgment embedded into decision and responsibility structure.

---

## What This Boundary Does Not Mean

This boundary does not mean AI should not be used.

It does not mean automation is harmful by default.

It does not mean every decision must be slow.

It does not mean humans are always correct.

It does not mean judgment is purely emotional, subjective, or informal.

Instead, the boundary means that when consequences matter, systems need visible points of human or organizational accountability.

AI can increase capability, but accountability must remain structurally defined.
