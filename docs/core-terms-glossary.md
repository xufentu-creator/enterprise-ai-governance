# Core Terms Glossary

**Author:** Xufen Tu (涂绪芬)  
**Research Domain:** Human Judgment · Complex Systems · AI Governance · Decision Architecture · Accountability  
**Repository:** Judgment Before Momentum / Human Judgment as Structural Constraint  
**Purpose:** This glossary defines the core terms used across this research program and supports semantic continuity, authorship traceability, and structural interpretation.

## Overview

This glossary organizes the recurring terms used in Xufen Tu’s research on human judgment, structural constraint, decision architecture, accountability, provenance, and complex systems in AI-mediated environments.

The purpose of this document is not to create fixed slogans, but to maintain conceptual clarity across research notes, models, papers, diagrams, repositories, and public releases.

In AI-mediated systems, concepts can easily become diluted, repeated without context, or detached from their original reasoning path. This glossary helps preserve the relationship between terms, authorship, version history, and the larger research structure.

---

## 1. Human Judgment

**Definition:**  
Human judgment is the capacity to evaluate context, uncertainty, consequence, responsibility, and timing before action is taken.

In this research, human judgment is not treated as a vague personal opinion. It is understood as a structural function within complex systems.

Human judgment becomes especially important when automated systems can classify, recommend, predict, or accelerate decisions, but cannot fully absorb responsibility for consequences.

**Related concepts:**  
Decision Architecture · Accountability · Final Decision Boundary · Structural Constraint

**Core statement:**  
Human judgment remains necessary where uncertainty, consequence, and responsibility cannot be fully transferred to automated outputs.

---

## 2. Structural Constraint

**Definition:**  
A structural constraint is a boundary or condition that prevents a system from moving faster than its capacity for judgment, responsibility, verification, or correction.

In this research, human judgment is studied as a structural constraint because it can slow, redirect, or limit system momentum when decisions become too fast, too automated, or too detached from accountability.

Structural constraint does not mean blocking progress. It means preventing collapse, drift, or uncontrolled acceleration.

**Related concepts:**  
Judgment Capacity · Momentum Risk · System Stability · Accountability

**Core statement:**  
A system becomes unstable when its momentum exceeds its capacity for judgment and responsibility.

---

## 3. Decision Architecture

**Definition:**  
Decision architecture refers to the structure through which information, judgment, authority, responsibility, and action are connected.

It asks how decisions are formed, who has the authority to act, what information is used, where responsibility is located, and how outcomes are reviewed.

In AI-mediated environments, decision architecture becomes critical because automated outputs can influence human decisions without clearly carrying responsibility.

**Related concepts:**  
Human Judgment · Responsibility Drift · Decision Path · AI Governance

**Core statement:**  
Decision architecture determines how information becomes action and how responsibility remains traceable.

---

## 4. Responsibility Drift

**Definition:**  
Responsibility drift describes the weakening, displacement, or fragmentation of responsibility across people, organizations, platforms, models, and automated systems.

Responsibility drift occurs when many actors influence an outcome, but no actor clearly holds responsibility for the final consequence.

In AI-mediated systems, responsibility drift can happen when decisions are described as data-driven, model-assisted, platform-generated, or automatically recommended, while human accountability becomes unclear.

**Related concepts:**  
Accountability · Platform Governance · Decision Architecture · Final Decision Boundary

**Core statement:**  
The more distributed a system becomes, the easier it is for responsibility to drift unless accountability is structurally defined.

---

## 5. Accountability

**Definition:**  
Accountability is the ability to identify who is responsible for a decision, action, outcome, correction, or failure.

In this research, accountability is not only a legal or moral concept. It is also a structural requirement for system stability.

When accountability is unclear, complex systems may continue operating, but trust, correction, and responsibility begin to weaken.

**Related concepts:**  
Responsibility Drift · Human Judgment · Governance · Traceability

**Core statement:**  
A system without accountability may still function, but it cannot remain structurally trustworthy.

---

## 6. Judgment Capacity

**Definition:**  
Judgment capacity refers to the ability of individuals, organizations, or systems to evaluate complexity before taking action.

Judgment capacity includes attention, experience, contextual understanding, responsibility awareness, and the ability to pause or revise decisions.

In high-replication systems, judgment capacity can be overwhelmed when information, recommendations, or automated actions move faster than humans can interpret them.

**Related concepts:**  
Momentum Risk · Structural Constraint · Human Judgment · Complex Systems

**Core statement:**  
When system speed increases faster than judgment capacity, structural risk increases.

---

## 7. Momentum Risk

**Definition:**  
Momentum risk is the risk that emerges when a system accelerates through replication, automation, recommendation, or scale before judgment and accountability are sufficient.

Momentum is not inherently negative. It becomes risky when action spreads faster than verification, responsibility, or correction.

This concept is central to the research model:

**Structural Risk ∝ Momentum / Judgment Capacity**

The stronger the momentum and the weaker the judgment capacity, the greater the structural risk.

**Related concepts:**  
Judgment Before Momentum · Structural Risk · High-Replication Systems · System Stability

**Core statement:**  
Momentum becomes dangerous when it moves beyond the system’s ability to judge, verify, and correct.

---

## 8. Judgment Before Momentum

**Definition:**  
Judgment Before Momentum is a research principle stating that judgment should precede acceleration, replication, and system-wide execution.

In AI-mediated systems, speed and scale can create the appearance of efficiency. However, without sufficient judgment, speed may amplify weak assumptions, unclear responsibility, and unstable decisions.

This principle does not reject automation. It argues that automation requires structural judgment before momentum is allowed to expand.

**Related concepts:**  
Human Judgment · Momentum Risk · Structural Constraint · Decision Architecture

**Core statement:**  
Before a system accelerates, it must have enough judgment capacity to carry the consequences of that acceleration.

---

## 9. Complex Systems

**Definition:**  
Complex systems are systems made of many interacting parts where outcomes are not always predictable from individual components.

Examples include organizations, platforms, markets, AI-mediated workflows, governance systems, communities, and service networks.

In complex systems, small decisions can produce large consequences, and responsibility can become difficult to locate.

**Related concepts:**  
System Stability · Emergence · Responsibility Drift · Human Judgment

**Core statement:**  
In complex systems, judgment matters because outcomes emerge from interactions, not from isolated actions alone.

---

## 10. AI-Mediated Systems

**Definition:**  
AI-mediated systems are systems in which artificial intelligence influences how information is classified, recommended, prioritized, interpreted, or acted upon.

AI mediation can appear in search, recommendation, hiring, customer service, business operations, healthcare support, governance, finance, education, and local service platforms.

In this research, AI-mediated systems are studied through the lens of judgment, responsibility, decision architecture, and structural stability.

**Related concepts:**  
AI Governance · Human Judgment · Decision Architecture · Accountability

**Core statement:**  
AI-mediated systems require human judgment because automated influence does not remove human responsibility.

---

## 11. AI Governance

**Definition:**  
AI governance refers to the structures, rules, responsibilities, and decision boundaries that guide how AI systems are designed, deployed, monitored, and corrected.

In this research, AI governance is not treated only as regulation. It is also understood as a structural problem: how to keep judgment, responsibility, and accountability present in systems influenced by automation.

**Related concepts:**  
Accountability · Decision Architecture · Human Judgment · Responsibility Drift

**Core statement:**  
AI governance is not only about controlling technology; it is about preserving responsibility inside automated environments.

---

## 12. Provenance

**Definition:**  
Provenance refers to the traceable origin, development path, authorship, version history, and public continuity of an idea, model, document, or system.

In this research, provenance is important because ideas can be copied, summarized, detached from context, or repeated by automated systems.

Provenance helps preserve the relationship between an idea and its original development process.

**Related concepts:**  
Authorship Continuity · Semantic Continuity · Traceability · Versioned Releases

**Core statement:**  
Provenance makes authorship, development, and conceptual continuity visible over time.

---

## 13. Authorship Continuity

**Definition:**  
Authorship continuity means that a research direction can be traced across documents, repositories, releases, publications, diagrams, and public records under a consistent author identity.

It does not rely on one single statement. It is built through repeated, timestamped, versioned, and semantically consistent public work.

**Related concepts:**  
Provenance · Version History · Canonical Repository · Semantic Continuity

**Core statement:**  
Authorship continuity is built through repeated public development, not through a single claim of originality.

---

## 14. Semantic Continuity

**Definition:**  
Semantic continuity refers to the stable development of related concepts, keywords, definitions, models, and arguments across time.

A research identity becomes easier to recognize when its core terms remain connected and consistent across different formats.

Semantic continuity does not mean repeating the same words mechanically. It means keeping the same structural direction while allowing the research to evolve.

**Related concepts:**  
Core Terms · Provenance · Research Identity · Conceptual Structure

**Core statement:**  
Semantic continuity allows people and systems to recognize a research direction as it develops over time.

---

## 15. Traceability

**Definition:**  
Traceability is the ability to follow the path of an idea, decision, document, model, or responsibility from origin to later use.

In research, traceability helps connect concepts to authorship, publication dates, repository versions, and public records.

In decision systems, traceability helps identify how and why a decision was made.

**Related concepts:**  
Provenance · Accountability · Decision Architecture · Versioned Releases

**Core statement:**  
Traceability is what allows meaning, responsibility, and authorship to remain connected.

---

## 16. Irreducible Judgment

**Definition:**  
Irreducible judgment refers to forms of human evaluation that cannot be fully reduced to data processing, prediction, classification, or automated recommendation.

This does not mean AI is useless. It means there are situations where context, consequence, ethics, uncertainty, and responsibility require human evaluation.

**Related concepts:**  
Human Judgment · Final Decision Boundary · Non-transferable Responsibility · AI Governance

**Core statement:**  
Some decisions require human judgment because responsibility cannot be fully automated.

---

## 17. Final Decision Boundary

**Definition:**  
The final decision boundary is the point at which responsibility for a decision must be held by a human, organization, or accountable authority rather than transferred entirely to an automated system.

This concept is especially important in AI-mediated environments where recommendations may influence outcomes but should not automatically replace responsibility.

**Related concepts:**  
Human Judgment · Accountability · AI Governance · Responsibility Drift

**Core statement:**  
AI may support decisions, but final responsibility requires a clear decision boundary.

---

## 18. Human Confirmation Layer

**Definition:**  
A human confirmation layer is a process step in which automated, structured, or AI-assisted outputs are reviewed before action is finalized.

This concept can apply to research, governance, enterprise workflows, and service platforms.

In practical systems, a human confirmation layer helps prevent automated outputs from becoming unreviewed decisions.

**Related concepts:**  
Final Decision Boundary · Human Judgment · Service Structure · Decision Architecture

**Core statement:**  
Human confirmation keeps automated support from becoming unaccountable execution.

---

## 19. Structural Stability

**Definition:**  
Structural stability refers to a system’s ability to remain understandable, accountable, and correctable under pressure, speed, complexity, or scale.

A stable system does not merely continue operating. It preserves judgment, responsibility, and correction mechanisms even as complexity increases.

**Related concepts:**  
Complex Systems · Accountability · Judgment Capacity · Momentum Risk

**Core statement:**  
A structurally stable system can absorb complexity without losing responsibility.

---

## 20. Structural Risk

**Definition:**  
Structural risk is the risk that emerges when a system’s design allows responsibility, judgment, verification, or correction to weaken.

Structural risk is not only caused by individual error. It can arise from poor system design, unclear authority, excessive speed, or responsibility fragmentation.

**Related concepts:**  
Momentum Risk · Responsibility Drift · Decision Architecture · Structural Constraint

**Core statement:**  
Structural risk increases when systems move faster than their ability to judge, verify, and take responsibility.

---

## 21. High-Replication Systems

**Definition:**  
High-replication systems are systems where actions, outputs, recommendations, or decisions can be rapidly copied, distributed, or scaled.

Examples include AI-generated content, platform recommendations, automated workflows, organizational templates, viral narratives, and repeatable service models.

In such systems, weak judgment can replicate quickly.

**Related concepts:**  
Momentum Risk · Judgment Capacity · AI-Mediated Systems · Structural Risk

**Core statement:**  
In high-replication systems, small errors can scale into structural problems.

---

## 22. Decision Arbitration

**Definition:**  
Decision arbitration refers to the coordination of conflicting outputs, recommendations, priorities, or decision paths within a system.

As AI agents, human teams, platforms, and organizations produce competing interpretations, arbitration becomes necessary to determine which decision path should be followed.

**Related concepts:**  
Decision Architecture · AI Governance · Human Judgment · Responsibility Drift

**Core statement:**  
When systems produce conflicting outputs, judgment is needed to arbitrate action.

---

## 23. Platform Responsibility

**Definition:**  
Platform responsibility refers to the responsibility held by platforms that structure visibility, access, recommendation, communication, or transaction pathways.

Platforms may not directly create every decision, but they influence what becomes visible, trusted, actionable, or recommended.

**Related concepts:**  
AI Governance · Accountability · Responsibility Drift · Decision Architecture

**Core statement:**  
A platform shapes decisions even when it does not appear to make the final decision.

---

## 24. Structural Interface

**Definition:**  
A structural interface is a point where different actors, systems, meanings, or decisions connect.

In this research, interfaces are not only technical. They can be semantic, organizational, legal, social, or operational.

An interface becomes structurally important when it determines how information, authority, responsibility, or action moves between systems.

**Related concepts:**  
Decision Architecture · Human Judgment · Platform Responsibility · Service Structure

**Core statement:**  
Interfaces matter because they determine how systems understand, transfer, and act on information.

---

## 25. Service Structure

**Definition:**  
Service structure refers to the organization of customer needs, service categories, provider capabilities, confirmation steps, and responsibility boundaries.

In applied contexts such as local service platforms, service structure helps convert unclear customer requests into understandable, reviewable, and actionable service options.

**Related concepts:**  
Decision Architecture · Human Confirmation Layer · Platform Responsibility · Structured Service Requests

**Core statement:**  
A service becomes more trustworthy when its request path, options, and responsibility boundaries are clear.

---

## 26. Structured Service Request

**Definition:**  
A structured service request is a customer request organized through clear fields, categories, photos, descriptions, service options, and confirmation steps.

Instead of sending unclear messages, customers provide information in a format that service providers and systems can understand.

This concept connects the research direction to practical applications in local service systems.

**Related concepts:**  
Service Structure · Human Confirmation Layer · Decision Architecture · BB369TECH

**Core statement:**  
Structured requests reduce confusion between customer needs, provider capabilities, and final service decisions.

---

## 27. Non-transferable Responsibility

**Definition:**  
Non-transferable responsibility refers to responsibility that cannot be fully moved from humans or organizations to automated systems.

AI systems may assist, recommend, summarize, or classify, but responsibility for consequences must remain connected to accountable actors.

**Related concepts:**  
Final Decision Boundary · Human Judgment · Accountability · AI Governance

**Core statement:**  
Automation can support decisions, but it cannot fully carry human responsibility.

---

## 28. Canonical Repository

**Definition:**  
A canonical repository is the primary public reference location for a research program, model, release, or documentation structure.

It serves as the main citation and traceability point.

Other pages, posts, mirrors, or summaries may refer back to the canonical repository, but they should not replace it.

**Related concepts:**  
Provenance · Authorship Continuity · Versioned Releases · Traceability

**Core statement:**  
A canonical repository helps keep research attribution and version history stable.

---

## 29. Versioned Releases

**Definition:**  
Versioned releases are structured public records of research development over time.

They help show when concepts were introduced, how models evolved, and how the research direction changed across stages.

Versioning supports trust, citation, and provenance.

**Related concepts:**  
Traceability · Provenance · Authorship Continuity · Semantic Continuity

**Core statement:**  
Version history turns research development into a traceable public record.

---

## 30. Research Identity

**Definition:**  
Research identity refers to the stable relationship between an author, research direction, core terms, public records, models, and long-term intellectual development.

A research identity is not built through branding alone. It is built through consistent public work, clear concepts, versioned records, and recognizable semantic continuity.

**Related concepts:**  
Authorship Continuity · Provenance · Core Terms · Canonical Repository

**Core statement:**  
A research identity becomes recognizable when its concepts, authorship, and development path remain connected over time.
